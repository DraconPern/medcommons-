House amendment sets off battle for MRI business

By Christopher Rowland, Globe Staff  |  June 11, 2004

House members have quietly approved a budget amendment that would help a politically connected family keep a lock on a large portion of the MRI business in the state, triggering a lobbying battle among doctors, hospitals, and businesses for a share in the state's lucrative medical imaging market.

Voted into the proposed budget with no hearings or public debate, the measure would prohibit doctors from referring patients to diagnostic facilities where they own an interest. Proponents say they are trying to curb an unethical practice and close loopholes in federal antikickback laws. The concern is that doctors could refer patients for unnecessary tests to profit from them.

But another immediate effect would be to freeze the number -- or even close down -- physician-owned MRIs in Massachusetts, opening the door for operators of stand-alone MRI clinics to consolidate or expand their share of one of the fastest-growing segments of the heath care business.

One potential major beneficiary of the measure would be the biggest single operator of MRI machines in the state, Shields Health Care Group, of Quincy, a privately held firm that operates 24 imaging facilities in Massachusetts. Shields operates more than a quarter of the machines tracked by the state.

The company's chairman and chief executive is Thomas F. Shields, who served on the Massachusetts Convention Center Authority and has been a frequent contributor to state and national political campaigns. Shields has fielded two lobbyists on Beacon Hill to push the MRI amendment. Groups representing the state's radiologists and community hospitals support the proposal.

Although the measure has come to be known as the "Shields amendment" around the State House, the company denied that it was behind the law.

"We did not initiate this legislation. We only responded when it came to our attention," said spokesman George Regan of Regan Communications Group in a prepared statement. "The people that attribute this to us are largely entrepreneurs and consultants who unfortunately have misguided doctors into following a federal loophole to get around the antikickback statute outlawing self-referrals."

Opponents say the amendment would give Shields too much power in the MRI market, and they denounced the lack of open debate about such a far-reaching issue.

"We found out about it a day or so after it was passed by the House, which is troublesome," said Dr. Baltej S. Maini, chief executive of Fallon Clinic, a Worcester-based physicians group that is planning to add MRI services for its patients in Central Massachusetts. "It was passed without any debate. It has massive implications on patient access and continuity of care, and it is totally under the radar."

The Massachusetts Medical Society, which represents doctors, is also lined up against the amendment. Another opponent, Harvard Vanguard Medical Associates, a major physician group in Boston, took the unusual step of mailing a letter to patients this week, urging them to contact top lawmakers and voice objections to the amendment.
