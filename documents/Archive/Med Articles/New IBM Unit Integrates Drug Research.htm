New IBM Unit Integrates Drug Research, Healthcare
by Nancy Weil, IDG News Service

IBM has started a new business unit focused on medicine within its life science division, promising to help customers integrate healthcare data, including research, genetic tests, patient medical records, clinical trials records, and medical images.

Operating as of Monday, the global unit is headed by vice president Mike Svinte, but IBM has not yet determined how many people will work in the unit. Svinte declined to reveal how much money IBM is investing in the "information-based medicine" unit but claimed it was "significant."

IBM has coined the phrase "information-based medicine" to describe its approach to integrating, mining, analyzing, and managing medical data from a variety of sources. The objective is to pull together information to provide researchers and doctors with better access to data with the goal of better patient care and drug development.

The unit will work with IBM partners that, for instance, offer life science applications for use with IBM software, including its middleware. Svinte offered as a partnership example IBM's work with deCODE Genetics, which has amassed enormous amounts of genetic, genealogical, and medical information on people in Iceland, where it is located. The two have paired to offer tools for analysis and data mining.

"What we see happening is the convergence between life sciences, healthcare, and IT," Svinte said. "The thought is that convergence really has the ability to change the way healthcare is delivered, to change the diagnosis and treatment."

Although IBM is already involved in projects that will be under the auspices of the information-based medicine unit, existing customers will find the start of that unit "transparent," Svinte said.

The field of information-based medicine is so new that a successful business model has not yet been clearly defined, and that could be a challenge IBM faces as it attempts to take a lead role, said Eric Brown, a vice president at Forrester Research in Cambridge, Mass.

The key question is who will pay for such data-integration systems. "What's the business model for these going forward?" Brown asked.
