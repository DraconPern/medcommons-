AAFP Nabs EHR Discounts, Ditches Open-Source Initiative
by Brian Reid, Health-IT World

The American Academy of Family Physicians has joined forces with nine vendors in an effort to bring high-quality, low-cost medical records technology and support to its members.

The move follows an ambitious, abandoned endeavor to develop an open-source electronic medical record -- based on software from MedPlexus -- after other physicians groups balked at joining AAFP in the effort. Now, the academy said it has turned its attention to finding interoperable solutions for its members that, while not free and open-source, will still speed the uptake of technology.

"We've had very significant meetings with a number of companies," said David Kibbe, the director of the AAFP Center for Health Information Technology. "They were telling us that it is very important for their companies ... that doctors get connected."

Out of those meetings has come an ambitious set of agreements, announced yesterday, that will make it easier for AAFP members to access technology. The agreements stretch from both smaller vendors that specialize in medical record technology to large IT players that are offering AAFP members an array of discounted services.

In addition to MedPlexus, which will offer discounted EHR software, A4 Health Systems , GE Medical Systems Information Technologies, NextGen, and Physician Micro Systems have all agreed to cut rates on their EHR technologies for AAFP members.

HP said it will offer discounts on a variety of technologies through a Web site specifically for academy members, and Quest Diagnostics' MedPlus said it will offer special prices on interface software and data management tools. Siemens said it will offer engineering services and managed hosting to AAFP, while medical instrument maker Welch Allyn said it will provide IT-enabled device technology to members of the academy.

The alliances are built on a four-part foundation that the AAFP has developed since abandoning its open-source EHR effort: affordability, compatibility, interoperability, and data stewardship. Doctors, Kibbe said, should have access to inexpensive solutions and "ought not to have to scrap every other piece of IT" in order to put in an EHR system. In addition, the academy is trying to ensure that different systems will be able to share data.

"The software may not be free," said Alan Zuckerman, an AAFP member from Georgetown University, "but we're making progress toward open systems."

AAFP said it expects the number of alliances to grow, and that it is being selective by ensuring that all four of its principles are met. "They're all agreeing to these principles," said Douglas Henley, the academy's executive vice president. "There are one or two other companies that wanted to do agreements with us, but they were not serious about questions of compatibility."

The vendor alliance marks the second major undertaking by AAFP this year in its quest toward easy-to-use medical records. The academy is a partner -- along with the Massachusetts Medical Society, Healthcare Information Management Systems Society, and ASTM International -- in an effort to create a so-called continuity of care record.
