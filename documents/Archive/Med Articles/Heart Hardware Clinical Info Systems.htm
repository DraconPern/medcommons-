Heart Hardware Talks to Clinical Info Systems
by Brian Reid, Health-IT World

Eclipsys scores two new clients, while GE and Guidant agree to work together toward a pacemaker that can talk to a clinical information system. Other pairings in this week's alliance-heavy roundup: IBM and UCSF, and Kodak buys Algotec. Corporate marriage announcements always welcome at brian_reid@health-itworld.com.

  • GE Medical Systems said it has allied with heart device powerhouse Guidant Corp. to develop tools that would allow Guidant implantable cardiovascular devices, such as pacemakers, to communicate with clinical information systems. The companies said their goal is to develop an open standard that would allow for medical device data to be automatically integrated into a patient's medical record. The effort builds on Guidant's existing Advanced Patient Management push, which seeks to incorporate additional information technology into its pacemakers, defibrillators, and cardiac resynchronization devices.
  • Two more hospitals will deploy Eclipsys' Sunrise XA clinical information system, the company said this week. Georgia's 315-bed Athens Regional Medical Center and San Antonio's University Health System, which includes a 478-bed hospital and a number of community clinics and offices, will both use the company's software, including the Sunrise computerized patient record. Eclipsys said it will also install its medication management and clinical documentation modules, while University Health will utilize Eclipsys' critical-care and ambulatory tools. The deals mark the biggest week of announcements for the clinical IT bellwether since the company announced problems with the SunriseXA workflow engine last month. Despite the company signing more deals, investors have pummeled the stock, which has dropped from $18.29 on Sept. 23 to $9.46 at the close yesterday.

In addition, Eclipsys and Sentillion said they would team up to incorporate Sentillion single-sign-on technology into Eclipsys products.

  • IBM and the University of California at San Francisco, said they will work together to make the reams of genetic information flowing from researchers more useful in the clinic. The school said the collaboration, which will initially focus on neurological diseases such as Alzheimer's, will give researchers a simple, Web-based interface with which they can mine data from across the institution, from clinical encounters to DNA chip results. To build the system, IBM experts will work on site with UCSF researchers, the school said.
  • Eastman Kodak beefed up its presence in the digital radiology arena by announcing it would buy Algotec Systems for $42.5 million. Privately-held Algotec is a developer of picture archiving and communications systems, and Kodak said it planned to use its new purchase as a "center of excellence" in creating PACS innovations.
