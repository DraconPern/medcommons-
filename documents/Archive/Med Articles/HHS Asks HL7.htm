HHS Asks HL7, Others to Focus on EHR Core Functions
by Brian Reid, Health-IT World

The Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) has weighed in on the Health Level Seven effort to create a standard model for electronic health records, writing HL7 to request that the group streamline the standard and focus on core functions of the record.

The two-page letter, from the co-chairs of HHS' Council on the Application of Health Information Technology, praised the HL7 effort and noted that "substantial benefits to the healthcare system would ensue" if the next version of the standard is approved by HL7 members in March.

Members of HL7's Electronic Health Record Special Interest Group (EHR-SIG), the working group charged with developing the new standard, said the HHS suggestions fit with discussions already ongoing. "This is really not that far from work already taking place," said Linda Fischetti, co-chair of the EHR-SIG. "This doesn't sound much different from what the EHR-SIG has already been working with."

The HHS letter focused primarily on ensuring that the standard proposed would not be unnecessarily complex, and it asked the group to leave concrete proposals for how the standard will apply to specific care settings for later consideration. The letter also said that "future program and policy developments related to EHRs will take into account the work of HL7," helping further assure the standard-setters that the standard will be not only approved but adopted.

Though HL7's work on the project comes at the behest of the government -- HHS secretary Tommy Thompson launched the formal EHR standard push last summer -- Fischetti said that HL7 rules require that all stakeholders be treated equally, and that the proposals floated by HHS aren't automatically given priority over other comments.

The comments from HHS come as other groups give HL7 additional feedback on the standard. The EHR Collaborative, a group of eight health technology and medical associations, will finish its series of four conference calls this week to help gather input from different medical communities. Additional reaction is expected over the next month.

The March ballot will be the second on the subject of EHRs from HL7. In September, the group's membership shot down the first version of the standards. The rejection, however, wasn't a surprise; members had to vote against the standards if they wanted to offer comments or refinements, no matter how minor.
