This is the radiology mantra. It is a Luddite approach to technology
that vastly oversimplifies the issue. The real problem is not having an
objective diagnosis and readily comparable treatment plans. Restricting
physician access to technology is not a prescription for success. For
example, many radiologists are now turning to interventional procedures
and even some cardiology. Should they be drummed out of the profession?
Why focus on imaging? What about orthopods doing unwarranted knee
replacements, neurosurgeons doing spinal fusions, urologists taking out
prostates? At least the imaging is causing no direct injury to the patient.

The only solution I see is for technology to provide clearly objective
results that leave no room for interpretation. Radiologists, if they are
involved should become one with the machine and the results should
completely speak for themselves. It is conceivable that the technology
is already so good that radiologists are doing more harm than good by
obfuscating what would otherwise be clear. For example, I believe that
having humans interpret screening mammograms is already a minor
disaster. The FDA has approved computer screening of PAP smears with no
human ever having to look at the negatives. Mammography should be next.
Then lung cancer. Then much of cardiology. Then some neuro-orthopedic
problems like the ones that Butler is studying.

I'll stop raving for now and try to keep this stuff mostly to myself.
