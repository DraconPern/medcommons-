Exclusive: e-MDs to Give Away Drug Reference Database
By Neil Versel, contributing editor

Ambulatory electronic medical records software vendor e-MDs will make its homegrown pharmaceutical reference database freely available as an open-source product, the company's chief executive said.

"I want to accelerate EMR adoption," e-MDs founder, president, and CEO David Winn, M.D., told Health-IT World News, confirming an informal announcement he made during the Toward an Electronic Patient Record (TEPR) conference last week.

The company is reviewing the accuracy of its reference materials and is hoping to make the information freely available by the middle of July, Winn said.

Winn added that e-MDs is opening up its database as a "goodwill gesture" to vendors selling to small and mid-size physician practices. The company mostly targets practices with fewer than 20 doctors; its largest customer has 44 physicians, according to Winn.

Vendors that license reference materials from other publishers normally pass on the licensing fees to their customers, but that can price their products out of the market for small practices, Winn said.

"One of the problems with EMR adoption is perceived cost," he said. "Physicians really are fixated on cost."

E-MDs began developing its own database for drug reference and interactions about five years ago as a way to hold down the cost of its EMR, according to Winn.

"I think we have probably 99% of the drugs out there [in the e-MDs database]," he said. "It's very comprehensive."

With an open-source product, users can provide feedback via a public Web site to help keep the database updated and accurate as new drugs and scientific research become available.

However, Winn does expect some backlash from two leading pharmaceutical reference publishers, Medi-Span and First Data Bank. Winn said that drug databases represent a "cash cow" for both companies.

A First Data Bank spokesperson had no comment. Officials from Medi-Span, a unit of Wolters Kluwer Health, were not available for comment.

Winn said that e-MDs eventually hopes to turn over distribution of the drug reference to one or more medical associations or government agencies. He said he has met with American Medical Association trustee Joseph Heyman, M.D., and Thomas Sullivan, M.D., immediate past president of the Massachusetts Medical Society -- both well-known advocates of clinical IT -- and that he has been approached by the federal Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality.

The AMA confirmed the meeting with Heyman but said that the organization has not received a formal proposal from e-MDs.
