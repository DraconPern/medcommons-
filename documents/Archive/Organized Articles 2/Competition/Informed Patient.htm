THE INFORMED PATIENT
By LAURA LANDRO

Push Grows for Online Health Data

A male office worker lowers his voice so colleagues won't hear the phone call to his doctor, but he finally has to say it loud enough for everyone to hear. "It's a rash...There's a lot of itching involved."

The humorous commercial is part of a campaign by Group Health Cooperative of Seattle to educate its 545,000 members about the advantages of going digital with medical records and doctor-patient communications.

Almost 98,000 members of the health plan have already signed up with the MyGroupHealth Web site, which lets them log on, privately and securely, any time of the day or night to make appointments for doctor visits, get lab results, and order drug refills online. No more embarrassing calls to doctors from the office. Patients also get online access to their records.

The campaign, which also includes print ads, is one of several new efforts to get consumers on board with the idea of wiring up the nation's health records. By replacing paper records with electronic networks, proponents say, patients will soon be able to manage their medical care and access their own health information much the way they bank and shop online now. Though General Electric has been running clever TV ads about the advantages of electronic medical records for more than a year as part of a corporate-image campaign, until now no one has taken the message directly to patients.

"The consumer has been completely left out of this equation, even though they have the biggest stake," says Brian Baum, a senior scholar at Duke University's Fuqua School of Business, which last week announced an ambitious campaign to gain consumer support for a national network of electronic medical records. "They have no concept of what an electronic medical record could mean for their health and the health of their family."

The American College of Medical Informatics, a group of medical professionals with expertise in information technology, is seeking support from consumer groups for a proposed campaign urging patients to ask their doctors if they use an electronic health record. One leader of that initiative is Paul Tang, chief medical information officer of the Palo Alto Medical Foundation, a pioneer in offering online-data access to its patients.

Dr. Tang, who chaired the government advisory Institute of Medicine committee on patient safety, which identified electronic medical records as critical to improving patient safety and reducing medical error, says the latest survey of Palo Alto's 20,000 users shows high satisfaction with the service, particularly because it saves time and office visits.

Dr. Tang says electronic records help doctors take better care of patients -- and patients take better care of themselves. The average doctor spends 38% of the time retrieving and entering information in the paper-based medical record, he notes, and even then 81% of the time can't find the information needed to make patient care decisions.

Relatively few Americans have electronic medical records now, but the pressure to adopt them widely has never been greater. President Bush called for national adoption of electronic records in his recent state of the union address. Though there are still issues to be resolved -- such as how much control patients should have over their own medical records -- technical standards groups are close to agreeing on uniform formats and other basics to lay the foundation for sharing information between doctors offices and hospitals. Some 60% of hospitals are either currently installing or plan to install a system to handle electronic medical records, according to a survey released at the recent annual meeting of the Health Information Management and Systems Society, an industry group. Though only about 5% of doctors use them, the American Academy of Family Physicians has teamed up with medical record suppliers to produce models that doctors can afford.

Proponents also point to potential public-health benefits. Duke's Fuqua School is proposing a "Health Data Exchange," which would include a national network of medical records that could be used to identify public-health threats and measure the effectiveness of care. Under its proposal, patients would provide their medical histories to the network through an Internet site, and updates to each patient medical record would occur automatically each time the patient seeks medical care or fills a prescription.

Hearing that, some consumers are likely to be alarmed about potential privacy violations. And while all patients should be concerned about privacy, there are protections being put into place regarding electronic medical records. Indeed, electronic medical records are probably more secure than the paper ones floating around different doctors' offices and hospitals -- and far easier to access in an emergency.

Under the new federal privacy law, there are strict rules governing the transfer of medical information electronically, and most records are password protected. With Duke's Health Data Exchange proposal, for instance, several groups would have access to anonymous medical histories, but only those with specific authorization from patients would be able to access a medical record with a name attached. And among hospitals using electronic medical records, nearly 70% say they use at least six security tools, including computer firewalls, user-access controls, multi-level passcodes and data encryption.

Until consumers are offered an electronic medical record, they won't appreciate its advantages. "The medical world is in the same situation that banking was a few years ago," says Pat Wise, who is leading the effort at Health Information Management and Systems Society to promote electronic medical records. "Before ATMs I was used to going to a bank and writing a check for cash, but then the bank introduced it to me and now I can't conceive of not having it."
