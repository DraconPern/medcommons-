PRS1-v1
January 23, 2004

Authorization Contexts
  1. LAN_DEFAULT - A (DICOM) Study object begins its MedCommons existence when it becomes available to the Router via a DICOM transfer. At that point, the study has no authorization context and is the HIPAA responsibility of whoever controls the LAN that the DICOM came over on. At this point, MedCommons has not modified the study in any way and may or may not have acknowledged receipt (e.g.: DICOM Storage Commitment or IHE). As long as MedCommons does not display the patient name or other Protected Health Information (PHI) there's no HIPAA issue. Obviously MedCommons tech support personnel may have access to PHI and, if they do, they have to be trained in the procedures surrounding this responsibility.
  2. LAN_CLERICAL - MedCommons may be called upon to display PHI as part of an activity list or worklist or as a result of a query. The typical LAN_CLERICAL context might be the front desk clerk at an imaging center. This person would be authorized to vew some or all of the recent activity on the Router but may not be authorized to view images or reports. Their HIPAA policies and training are the responsibility of whoever controls the DICOM LAN. Occasionally, there may be some default restrictions placed upon the LAN_CLERICAL context - e.g.: there are two imaging facilities on the East and West sides of town and the clerk at each facility is restricted, by default to one or the other. Even so, note that the study has not been modified yet.
    1. If MedCommons displays PHI (E.G.: on it's Main Screen) in this context, we will be expected to have a HIPAA Log entry that includes Time/Date, Object ID, LAN ID and UserID. The Patient's approval is implied and may have been documented in something they signed when they agreed to the scan. In the rare situation where the Patient is being scanned anonymously (e.g.: an HIV test or the patient is unidentified) MedCommons should provide tools to identify a particular object without relying on PHI. Even so, a HIPAA Log entry should be created.
  3. LAN_CLINICAL - Typically, this is the most common context. It allows full access by technologists, physicians and nurses. This context also applies to the default DICOM received and does not require any particular modification of the study or associated information.
  4. PATIENT_ANY - Here, the patient has unequivocal and possibly sole access to the PHY (e.g.: the equivalent of being imaged as John Doe and being handed the negatives to take away). The patient, once they identify themselves somehow, take full control of the study. For example, the patient might supply a password or PKI certificate that is used to encrypt the study and all PHI. Alternatively, a CD-ROM could be handed to the patient and all PHI erased from the router. A LAN_CLERICAL or LAN_CLINICAL transaction may precede PATIENT_ANY.
  5. LICENSED_CLINICIAN - This is the most common "second opinion" context where an imaging facility might try to limit its liability by insisting that PHI and images are accessed only by a patient's physician. The facility that controls the study will typically depend on the policies associed with the license to limit its liability. For example, any physician with a valid license and DEA number can prescribe narcotics. In this example, MedCommos might be playing the role of pharmacy and be subject to HIPAA and related regulations that control physician authentication and we, in turn, may look to a third-party service (e.g.: Tracmed) to take that responsibility.
  6. BUSINESS_ASSOCIATE - This is another common situation where MedCommons is used to move or copy a study to another HIPAA-compliant facility such as a 3D processing service. Business Associate is a HIPAA-defined category that is separate from the Provider category. Business Associates have a contractual relationship with the entity that controls the study. I'm not sure whether MedCommons will need to keep separate contexts for Business Associates vs. Providers (hospitals) but it can't hurt.
  7. EMERGENCY - The specified contexts  where MedCommons might  override other contexts.
  8. NAMED_CLINICIAN - An alternative to LICENSED_CLINICIAN. Authentication might be via the USPS and Authentidate.
  9. AUTHORIZATION_REQUIRED - This context might be assigned to a study that is being placed into archival storage or simply to allow listing in a worklist at a remote site. The controlling entity would insist that it be contacted before release of the study. Encryption, may or may not be a component of the implementation. If encryption is used, then we will have to deal with back doors and key escrow issues.
  10. STRONG_AUTHENTICATION - This context could be applied to force users to be biometrically authenticated or bonded in some way.
  11. ROLE_SENSITIVE - This can of worms opens when authorization needs to depend on the recipient's role. I'm not aware of any standard or legal definition of roles in healthcare or HIPAA and things get even more complicated when patient's family and health care proxies are involved. I'd like to stay away from this if at all possible.
